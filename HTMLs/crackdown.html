Landmarks Cuisine Scenary

Growing influence of China

British Colonial Period (1842 - 1997)

  • 1842: The Treaty of Nanking cedes Hong Kong Island to Britain after the First Opium War.
  • 1898: The New Territories are leased to Britain for 99 years, expanding Hong Kong's territory.
  • Post-WWII: Hong Kong experiences rapid economic growth, becoming a global financial center.
  • 1984: The Sino-British Joint Declaration is signed, outlining the "One Country, Two Systems" principle and paving the way for the handover.

Handover to China (1997)

  • 1997: Hong Kong is handed back to China under the principle of "One Country, Two Systems," ensuring a high degree of autonomy.
  • 1997: The Provisional Legislative Council is established, composed mainly of pro-Beijing members.

Post-Handover Period (Late 1990s - Early 2000s):

  • 1997-2000: International concerns about Hong Kong's autonomy and human rights issues arise.
  • 1997-2002: Tung Chee-hwa becomes the first Chief Executive of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region (HKSAR).
  • 1999: The Court of Final Appeal is established, ensuring judicial independence.
  • 2003: Massive protests against the proposed anti-subversion legislation (Article 23) lead to its withdrawal.
  • 2005: The government introduces a political reform package to expand the Chief Executive election committee.

Recent years

  • 2010: The government introduces political reforms to increase the number of directly elected Legislative Council seats.
  • 2014: The "Umbrella Movement" protests demand universal suffrage for the Chief Executive election.
  • 2019: Protests erupt against the proposed extradition bill, eventually leading to its withdrawal.
  • 2020: The National Security Law is imposed, criminalizing secession, subversion, terrorism, and collusion with foreign forces.
  • 2019-2020: Global reactions to the protests include statements of support for Hong Kong's autonomy and criticism of China's handling of the situation.
  • 2021: Authorities arrest numerous pro-democracy activists under the National Security Law.
  • 2021: Countries impose sanctions in response to the National Security Law, straining international relations.
  • 2021: The electoral system is overhauled with changes that reduce the number of directly elected seats in the Legislative Council.

Overview

Before Hong Kong's transition to Chinese rule in 1997, assurances were given that rights and freedoms would be safeguarded under Hong Kong and international law. However, over the following two decades, despite peaceful advocacy for these rights, both the Hong Kong and Beijing governments consistently retreated from these commitments.

In February 2019, Hong Kong authorities proposed amendments that would allow the extradition of criminal suspects to mainland China, where due process is often ignored. This proposal sparked extensive and largely peaceful protests from June onward. While the authorities abandoned the extradition changes, they have not addressed other demands, such as an investigation into police use of excessive force, and have imposed new restrictions on freedom of expression and assembly.

As of 2021, some countries, including the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, and the European Union, have taken actions such as imposing travel bans and asset freezes on officials involved in the National Security Law. These measures were often framed as a response to perceived human rights violations and erosion of autonomy in Hong Kong.

Protest events (From the handover onwards)

1997 Handover

  • Handover ceremony on July 1, 1997.
  • Initial protests expressing concerns about the future of Hong Kong's autonomy under "one country, two systems."

2003 Anti-Subversion Law Protest (Article 23)

  • Massive protests in July 2003 against proposed Article 23 legislation.
  • Concerns over the potential suppression of political dissent.
  • Government withdrawal of the bill in response to public opposition.

2012 National Education Protests

  • Demonstrations in 2012 opposing mandatory national education curriculum.
  • Criticisms of the curriculum for promoting a pro-Beijing view of history.
  • Government decision not to make the curriculum compulsory.

2014 Umbrella Movement (Occupy Central)

  • September 2014 protests demanding genuine universal suffrage for Chief Executive election.
  • Occupations in key areas (Admiralty, Mong Kok, Causeway Bay) for 79 days.
  • Lasting impact on Hong Kong's political landscape, despite immediate goals not being achieved.

2019 Anti-Extradition Bill Protests

  • June 2019 protests against proposed extradition bill to mainland China.
  • Evolving demands, including an independent inquiry into police conduct and universal suffrage.
  • Millions participating in marches, strikes, and civil disobedience.

2020 National Security Law Protests

  • Imposition of the national security law by China in June 2020.
  • Criminalization of secession, subversion, terrorism, and collusion with foreign forces.
  • Protests continuing, but with a chilling effect on dissent.

2021 Electoral Changes Protests

  • Early 2021 electoral changes reducing directly elected seats in the Legislative Council.
  • Apple Daily, a pro-democracy newspaper, ceases operations in June due to financial pressures, asset freezes, and the government's actions. Apple Daily played a crucial role during this period as a pro-democracy media outlet. Its closure marked a significant blow to press freedom and media diversity in Hong Kong. The newspaper provided a platform for critical reporting on the Chinese and Hong Kong governments, contributing to public awareness and shaping the narrative during a tumultuous period of protests and political changes.
  • Criticisms of the changes as further restricting democratic representation.
  • Protests and international criticism in response to the electoral changes.

2022

  • Former security chief John Lee is installed as Hong Kong's chief executive following what was widely seen as a sham election in which he was the only candidate. Lee is among a number of Hong Kong and mainland Chinese officials under U.S. and European visa bans for their roles in cracking down on human rights in the territory.
  • Authorities require new textbooks asserting that Hong Kong was never a British colony, claiming China never recognized the treaties over its status. The move is seen as an attempt to erase the memory of past freedoms and assert the ideology of China's great rejuvenation" that is central to Xi's political agenda.

Apple Daily

Apple Daily is a pro-democracy tabloid-style newspaper in Hong Kong known for its openly critical reporting on the Chinese and Hong Kong governments.

Founder: Jimmy Lai, a prominent pro-democracy activist and media entrepreneur.

The newspaper and its founder faced increased scrutiny and legal actions from the Hong Kong government. Jimmy Lai was arrested, and Apple Daily's offices were raided multiple times.

The closure of Apple Daily (2021) was seen as a significant blow to press freedom and media diversity in Hong Kong. It raised concerns about the shrinking space for dissenting voices and the ability of independent media to operate freely.
